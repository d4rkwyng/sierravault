# Timeline 1980–1999

<small style="color: gray">Last updated: May 11, 2026</small>

## Overview

This is the year-by-year chronicle of Sierra On-Line's independent era — from the May 1980 release of [[1980 - Hi-Res Adventure 1 - Mystery House|Mystery House]] (founded the company financially) through the February 1999 closure of the original Oakhurst studio under [[Corporate Lineage|Havas Interactive ownership]]. For the post-1999 corporate-and-IP arc, see [[Corporate Lineage]]; for the engine technical evolution, see [[Engine History]] (planned).

The narrative spine is releases, but each year's entry also notes corporate events, technical milestones, and personnel changes. Releases listed are flagship or first-of-kind titles; full year-by-year release lists are in the [[Site Index]].

---

## 1980

**Founding year.** [[Ken Williams]] and [[Roberta Williams]] found **On-Line Systems** in Simi Valley, California. The first product is a FORTRAN compiler for the TRS-80; the second is Roberta's [[1980 - Hi-Res Adventure 1 - Mystery House|Mystery House]] (May 1980), the first home computer adventure game with graphics.[^ref-1][^ref-2]

**Major releases:** [[1980 - Hi-Res Adventure 1 - Mystery House|Mystery House]], [[1980 - Hi-Res Adventure 2 - The Wizard and the Princess|The Wizard and the Princess]], [[1980 - Hi-Res Adventure 0 - Mission Asteroid|Mission Asteroid]], [[1980 - Hi-Res Cribbage|Hi-Res Cribbage]], [[1980 - Hi-Res Football|Hi-Res Football]], [[1980 - Hi-Res Soccer|Hi-Res Soccer]].

**Technical:** *Mystery House* runs on the Apple II using vector-drawn graphics layered over a text-parser interface.[^ref-3]

## 1981

The company relocates to Coarsegold (later [[Yosemite Entertainment|Oakhurst]]) at the foothills of the Sierra Nevada. [[Warren Schwader]] is hired as On-Line Systems' first programmer.[^ref-4]

**Major releases:** [[1981 - Hi-Res Adventure 3 - Cranston Manor|Cranston Manor]], [[1981 - Hi-Res Adventure 4 - Ulysses and the Golden Fleece|Ulysses and the Golden Fleece]], [[1981 - Threshold|Threshold]] (Warren Schwader's Apple II arcade game; sold ~25,000 copies), [[1981 - Softporn Adventure|Softporn Adventure]] (text-only adult title), [[1981 - Crossfire|Crossfire]], [[1981 - Frogger|Frogger]] (Apple II port).

## 1982

**Rebrand:** On-Line Systems is renamed **Sierra On-Line, Inc.**, taking the name from the surrounding mountain range. The company is now the largest independent computer-game publisher in the US.[^ref-5][^ref-6]

**Major releases:** [[1982 - Hi-Res Adventure 5 - Time Zone|Time Zone]] (a massive 6-disk adventure, ambitious for the Apple II), [[1981 - Jawbreaker|Jawbreaker]], [[1981 - Frogger|Frogger (Atari 2600 port)]], [[1982 - Mouskattack|Mouskattack]], [[1982 - Pest Patrol|Pest Patrol]].

## 1983

The video-game industry **crash of 1983** begins, devastating cartridge-based publishers but also hitting computer-game companies. Sierra would shrink from over 100 staff to roughly 35 by mid-1984.[^ref-7]

**Major releases:** [[1983 - Hi-Res Adventure 6 - The Dark Crystal|The Dark Crystal]] (last Hi-Res Adventures entry), [[1983 - Troll's Tale|Troll's Tale]], [[1983 - Sammy Lightfoot|Sammy Lightfoot]] (Warren Schwader; introduced page-flipping animation technique still in use today), Frogger II, [[1983 - Mine Shaft|Mine Shaft]], [[1983 - Early Math|Early Math]], [[1983 - Learning with Leeper|Learning with Leeper]].

## 1984

**The Sierra rescue:** IBM commissions [[1984 - King's Quest - Quest for the Crown|King's Quest]] (May 1984) to showcase the new IBM PCjr. Designed by [[Roberta Williams]] and using the new [[Adventure Game Interpreter|AGI]] engine, *King's Quest* is the first computer adventure with animated graphics, a freely walking on-screen character, and text-parser input. It establishes the King's Quest series, the AGI engine line, and Sierra's identity for the next decade.[^ref-8][^ref-9]

**Major releases:** [[1984 - King's Quest - Quest for the Crown|King's Quest: Quest for the Crown]], [[1984 - Wizard of Id's WizMath|Wizard of Id's WizMath]], [[1984 - Wizard of Id's WizType|Wizard of Id's WizType]], [[1984 - Mickey's Space Adventure|Mickey's Space Adventure]], [[1984 - Gelfling Adventure|Gelfling Adventure]] (licensed from *The Dark Crystal*), [[1984 - Learning with FuzzyWOMP|Learning with FuzzyWOMP]].

## 1985

The AGI engine matures; Sierra ports *King's Quest* to many platforms (Mac, Amiga, Atari ST, Tandy 1000, Apple II, PC).

**Major releases:** [[1985 - King's Quest II - Romancing the Throne|King's Quest II: Romancing the Throne]] (Roberta Williams), [[1985 - Sierra Championship Boxing|Sierra Championship Boxing]], [[1985 - Stunt Flyer|Stunt Flyer]], [[1985 - Thexder|Thexder]] (Game Arts of Japan, Sierra-localized).

## 1986

**Major releases:** [[1986 - King's Quest III - To Heir Is Human|King's Quest III: To Heir Is Human]] (Roberta Williams; first KQ with a non-royal protagonist), [[1986 - Space Quest - The Sarien Encounter|Space Quest: The Sarien Encounter]] (Mark Crowe & Scott Murphy debut; Sierra's first sci-fi adventure), [[1986 - The Black Cauldron|The Black Cauldron]] (licensed from Disney), [[1984 - Donald Duck's Playground|Donald Duck's Playground]] (Al Lowe debut).

## 1987

Sierra's "Two Guys from Andromeda" ([[Mark Crowe]] and [[Scott Murphy]]) and [[Jim Walls]] cement the company's franchise model — multiple lead designers each owning a distinct series.

**Major releases:** [[1987 - Mixed-Up Mother Goose|Mixed-Up Mother Goose]] (Roberta Williams), [[1987 - Police Quest - In Pursuit of the Death Angel|Police Quest: In Pursuit of the Death Angel]] (Jim Walls; first Sierra adult-targeted procedural drama), [[1987 - Leisure Suit Larry in the Land of the Lounge Lizards|Leisure Suit Larry in the Land of the Lounge Lizards]] (Al Lowe; remake of *Softporn Adventure* with graphics), [[1987 - Space Quest II - Vohaul's Revenge|Space Quest II: Vohaul's Revenge]], [[1987 - The Ancient Art of War at Sea|The Ancient Art of War at Sea]] (strategy).

## 1988

**Sierra IPO** — December 7, 1988, Sierra On-Line, Inc. goes public on NASDAQ under ticker SIER.[^ref-10] [[Sierra Creative Interpreter|SCI]] (SCI0) replaces AGI as the company's flagship adventure-game engine, introducing 256-color VGA support and a much richer scripting language.[^ref-11]

**Major releases:** [[1988 - King's Quest IV - The Perils of Rosella|King's Quest IV: The Perils of Rosella]] (Roberta Williams; ships in both AGI and SCI versions during the engine transition; first computer game with a Hollywood-orchestra MIDI score), [[1988 - Police Quest II - The Vengeance|Police Quest II: The Vengeance]] (Jim Walls), [[1988 - Leisure Suit Larry Goes Looking for Love (in Several Wrong Places)|Leisure Suit Larry 2]], [[1988 - Manhunter - New York|Manhunter: New York]] (Evryware; Sierra-published), [[1988 - Silpheed|Silpheed]] (Game Arts; Sierra-localized).

## 1989

The watershed year. SCI matures; Sierra ships flagship adventures across all its franchises. [[Hoyle Series|Hoyle]] launches as Sierra's casual-games arm.

**Major releases:** [[1989 - The Colonel's Bequest|The Colonel's Bequest]] (Roberta Williams; founds Laura Bow series), [[1989 - Space Quest III - The Pirates of Pestulon|Space Quest III: The Pirates of Pestulon]] (first Sierra game with a CD-quality MIDI score), [[1989 - Leisure Suit Larry III - Passionate Patti in Pursuit of the Pulsating Pectorals|Leisure Suit Larry III]], [[1989 - Quest for Glory I - So You Want to Be a Hero|Quest for Glory I: So You Want to Be a Hero]] (Lori & Corey Cole; founds the Quest for Glory RPG-adventure hybrid series), [[1989 - Manhunter - San Francisco|Manhunter: San Francisco]], [[1989 - Hoyle Official Book of Games - Volume 1|Hoyle Official Book of Games: Volume 1]] (Warren Schwader; founds the Hoyle franchise), [[1988 - Gold Rush|Gold Rush!]] (Doug MacNeill), [[1989 - A-10 Tank Killer|A-10 Tank Killer]] ([[Dynamix]] under Sierra publishing).

## 1990

**Acquisition:** Sierra acquires [[Dynamix]] (Eugene, Oregon) in 1990, bringing flight simulators and action games under the Sierra umbrella.[^ref-12]

**Major releases:** [[1990 - King's Quest V - Absence Makes the Heart Go Yonder|King's Quest V: Absence Makes the Heart Go Yonder]] (Roberta Williams; first Sierra adventure to abandon text parser entirely for point-and-click; first multimedia CD-ROM Sierra title in 1991 enhanced version), [[1990 - Conquests of Camelot - The Search for the Grail|Conquests of Camelot]] (Christy Marx), [[1990 - Quest for Glory II - Trial by Fire|Quest for Glory II: Trial by Fire]], [[1990 - Roberta Williams' King's Quest I - Quest for the Crown|King's Quest I VGA Remake]], [[1990 - Mixed-Up Mother Goose (SCI Remake)|Mixed-Up Mother Goose SCI]], [[1990 - Stellar 7|Stellar 7]] (Dynamix), [[1990 - Hoyle Official Book of Games - Volume 2|Hoyle Volume 2]].

## 1991

The Multimedia CD-ROM era begins; *King's Quest V* on CD-ROM is one of Sierra's first major CD releases with full speech.

**Major releases:** [[1991 - Space Quest IV - Roger Wilco and the Time Rippers|Space Quest IV: Roger Wilco and the Time Rippers]], [[1991 - Police Quest III - The Kindred|Police Quest III: The Kindred]], [[1991 - Conquests of the Longbow - The Legend of Robin Hood|Conquests of the Longbow: The Legend of Robin Hood]], [[1991 - EcoQuest - The Search for Cetus|EcoQuest: The Search for Cetus]], [[1991 - Heart of China|Heart of China]] (Dynamix), [[1991 - The Adventures of Willy Beamish|The Adventures of Willy Beamish]] (Dynamix), [[1991 - Castle of Dr. Brain|Castle of Dr. Brain]] (Corey Cole; founds Dr. Brain series).

## 1992

**Acquisition:** Sierra acquires [[Bright Star Technology]] (educational software).[^ref-13]

**Major releases:** [[1992 - King's Quest VI - Heir Today, Gone Tomorrow|King's Quest VI: Heir Today, Gone Tomorrow]] (Jane Jensen + Roberta Williams; widely regarded as the high point of Sierra's adventure-game era; first Sierra title with multiple endings), [[1992 - The Dagger of Amon Ra|The Dagger of Amon Ra]] (Bruce Balfour, second Laura Bow), [[1993 - Space Quest V - The Next Mutation|Space Quest V: The Next Mutation]] (Mark Crowe + Stephen Cole), [[1991 - Leisure Suit Larry 5 - Passionate Patti Does a Little Undercover Work|Leisure Suit Larry V]] (Al Lowe).

## 1993

**Acquisition:** Sierra acquires French publisher [[Coktel Vision]] (Gobliiins, Lost in Time, Inca).[^ref-14] The Sierra Network (later renamed The ImagiNation Network) launches as an early online gaming service.[^ref-15]

**Major releases:** [[1993 - Gabriel Knight - Sins of the Fathers|Gabriel Knight: Sins of the Fathers]] (Jane Jensen; founds Gabriel Knight series), [[1993 - Police Quest - Open Season|Police Quest IV: Open Season]] (Daryl F. Gates), [[1992 - Quest for Glory III - Wages of War|Quest for Glory III: Wages of War]], [[1993 - Freddy Pharkas - Frontier Pharmacist|Freddy Pharkas: Frontier Pharmacist]] (Al Lowe + Josh Mandel), [[1993 - Pepper's Adventures in Time|Pepper's Adventures in Time]] (Bill Davis; final EcoQuest-sibling), [[1993 - EcoQuest - Lost Secret of the Rainforest|EcoQuest 2: Lost Secret of the Rainforest]], [[1993 - Betrayal at Krondor|Betrayal at Krondor]] (Dynamix; based on Raymond E. Feist novels), [[1993 - Aces Over Europe|Aces Over Europe]] (Dynamix), [[1993 - Hoyle Classic Card Games|Hoyle Classic Card Games]] (Warren Schwader's final Sierra title).

## 1994

The SCI32 engine (SCI 2.x) ships, supporting larger sprites and 32-bit colour for Windows.[^ref-16]

**Major releases:** [[1994 - King's Quest VII - The Princeless Bride|King's Quest VII: The Princeless Bride]] (Roberta Williams + Mark Seibert; first Sierra adventure to abandon AGI/SCI parser conventions for a fully animated cartoon interface), [[1994 - Outpost|Outpost]] (Bruce Balfour; over-promised colony-management sim).

## 1995

**Acquisitions:** [[Impressions Games]] (UK; Caesar, Pharaoh, Lords of the Realm), [[Papyrus Design Group]] (NASCAR Racing).[^ref-17][^ref-18]

**Major releases:** [[1995 - Phantasmagoria|Phantasmagoria]] (Roberta Williams; 7 CD-ROM disks, live-action video, banned in several countries; one of Sierra's best-selling titles), [[1995 - The Beast Within - A Gabriel Knight Mystery|The Beast Within: A Gabriel Knight Mystery]] (Jane Jensen; full-motion video sequel to GK1), [[1995 - Shivers|Shivers]] (Marcia Bales), [[1995 - Torin's Passage|Torin's Passage]] (Al Lowe; outside-LSL family game), [[1995 - Police Quest - SWAT|Police Quest: SWAT]] (full-motion video police simulator; founds the SWAT spin-off series), [[1995 - Caesar II|Caesar II]] (Impressions).

## 1996

**The pivot.** On July 24, 1996, [[Corporate Lineage|CUC International announces its USD 1.06 billion acquisition of Sierra]] in stock. Deal closes February 21, 1997. [[Ken Williams]] departs in late 1996; cost-cutting and creative-control conflicts begin.[^ref-19][^ref-20] Sierra acquires [[Synergistic Software]].[^ref-21]

**Major releases:** [[1996 - Lighthouse - The Dark Being|Lighthouse: The Dark Being]] (Jon Bock), [[1996 - Phantasmagoria - A Puzzle of Flesh|Phantasmagoria: A Puzzle of Flesh]] (Lorelei Shannon), [[1996 - MissionForce - Cyberstorm|MissionForce: Cyberstorm]] (Dynamix), [[1996 - Leisure Suit Larry 7 - Love for Sail|Leisure Suit Larry: Love for Sail!]] (Al Lowe; final LSL of the Sierra era for over a decade).

## 1997

The CUC consolidation deepens. The Oakhurst studio still operates but staff are being cut.

**Major releases:** [[1995 - The Beast Within - A Gabriel Knight Mystery|GK2 re-releases]], [[1997 - Shivers Two - Harvest of Souls|Shivers Two: Harvest of Souls]], [[1997 - Betrayal in Antara|Betrayal in Antara]] (Dynamix), [[1997 - Diablo - Hellfire|Diablo: Hellfire]] (Sierra-published Blizzard expansion).

## 1998

**Major releases:** [[1998 - King's Quest - Mask of Eternity|King's Quest: Mask of Eternity]] (Roberta Williams + Mark Seibert; controversial 3D action-RPG hybrid; Roberta's last Sierra game), [[1998 - Quest for Glory V - Dragon Fire|Quest for Glory V: Dragon Fire]] (Lori & Corey Cole; final QFG entry), [[1993 - Police Quest - Open Season|Police Quest 4 re-releases]], [[1998 - Half-Life|Half-Life]] (Valve; Sierra-funded, Sierra-published — one of the most influential FPS games of all time).

**Corporate:** In June, Cendant Software is sold to Havas SA for ~USD 800M after the Cendant accounting scandal.[^ref-22] Sierra is now under [[Corporate Lineage|Havas Interactive]] ownership.

## 1999

**Closure year.** In February 1999, Havas Interactive closes the original Sierra Oakhurst studio. Development is consolidated to Bellevue, Washington (under the [[Yosemite Entertainment]] name) and other Vivendi/Havas sites.[^ref-23] [[Roberta Williams]] retires from game development.[^ref-24]

**Major releases:** [[1999 - Homeworld|Homeworld]] ([[Relic Entertainment]]; Sierra-published; pioneering 3D space RTS), [[1999 - SWAT 3 - Close Quarters Battle|SWAT 3: Close Quarters Battle]] (Yosemite), [[1999 - Half-Life - Opposing Force|Half-Life: Opposing Force]] (Gearbox; Sierra-published), [[1998 - Caesar III|Caesar III]] (Impressions), [[1999 - Hoyle Casino|Hoyle Casino]] (first dedicated casino-only entry), [[1999 - NASCAR Racing 3|NASCAR Racing 1999 Edition]] (Papyrus), [[1999 - Starsiege|Starsiege]] (Dynamix), [[1997 - Driver's Education '98|Driver's Education '99]].

The independent Sierra era ends with the studio closure. The brand continues under Havas/Vivendi/Activision/Microsoft ownership — see [[Corporate Lineage]] for the post-1999 narrative.

## Personnel arc summary

- **Founded the company** (1979–1980): [[Ken Williams]], [[Roberta Williams]]
- **First hires** (1980–1981): [[Warren Schwader]], [[Jeff Stephenson]]
- **Tier-1 designers** during the 1984–1996 golden era: [[Roberta Williams]], [[Al Lowe]], [[Mark Crowe]], [[Scott Murphy]], [[Jane Jensen]], [[Lori Ann Cole]], [[Corey Cole]], [[Jim Walls]], [[Christy Marx]], [[Bruce Balfour]], [[Lorelei Shannon]]
- **Engine and SCI architecture**: [[Jeff Stephenson]] (chief SCI architect), [[Bob Heitman]], [[Mark Seibert]]
- **CUC-era departures** (1996–1998): [[Ken Williams]] (1996), most senior leadership over the next two years
- **Survivors into Havas/Vivendi**: [[Mark Seibert]] (Yosemite Entertainment lead through SWAT 3), [[Lorelei Shannon]] (Phantasmagoria 2), [[Roberta Williams]] (through Mask of Eternity 1998)
