# Jerry Moore

<small style="color: gray">Last updated: January 30, 2026 | Pipeline research: January 31, 2026</small>

## Overview

Jerry Moore is an American programmer and artist who worked at Sierra On-Line during the 1980s and early 1990s.[^ref-1] His Sierra career showcases the versatility that characterized many early game developers, as he transitioned from programming on early AGI engine games to creating artwork for later SCI-era productions.[^ref-2] Moore's credits span from the educational title Donald Duck's Playground (1984) to The Island of Dr. Brain (1992), reflecting Sierra's evolution from simple graphics to sophisticated VGA adventures.

Moore's dual expertise in programming and art was valuable during an era when small development teams required individuals who could contribute across disciplines.[^ref-1] His work helped bring Sierra's educational and children's titles to life, contributing to games that introduced young players to computing.

**Note:** Moore's known Sierra credits are limited to just two games spanning an eight-year period, suggesting he may have worked on additional projects with uncredited contributions, or had a more sporadic involvement with the company compared to other Sierra developers.

## Career

### Programming Era

Moore's earliest Sierra work came as a programmer on the AGI port of Donald Duck's Playground (1984), Sierra's acclaimed educational game designed by [[Al Lowe]].[^ref-3] Working alongside Doug MacNeill, Moore helped adapt the game for Sierra's Adventure Game Interpreter engine, which required different programming approaches than the original Commodore 64 version.[^ref-3] The AGI engine's limitations for non-adventure games made this port particularly challenging, as Al Lowe himself described certain aspects as "ridiculous."[^ref-4]

### Transition to Art

By the early 1990s, Moore had transitioned to an artist role, contributing to Sierra's increasingly sophisticated visual productions.[^ref-2] This career evolution was not unusual at Sierra, where talented individuals often moved between disciplines as the company's needs and their own skills developed.

### The Island of Dr. Brain (1992)

Moore served as an artist on [[1992 - The Island of Dr. Brain\|The Island of Dr. Brain]], the second entry in Sierra's educational puzzle adventure series.[^ref-2] Working alongside Bill Davis and Jennell Jaquays, he helped create the whimsical visual style that characterized Dr. Brain's eccentric island laboratory.[^ref-5] The game's 256-color VGA graphics required careful artistic implementation to balance educational content with visual appeal.

## Notable Works

### Donald Duck's Playground (1984)

On [[1984 - Donald Duck's Playground\|Donald Duck's Playground]], Moore contributed programming for the AGI engine adaptation, helping bring Al Lowe's Disney-licensed educational game to additional platforms.[^ref-3] The game taught children about money management through mini-games where Donald Duck works various jobs to build a playground for his nephews. The AGI port required creative solutions to adapt the game's mechanics to an engine designed primarily for text-parser adventure games.[^ref-4]

### The Island of Dr. Brain (1992)

As an artist on [[1992 - The Island of Dr. Brain\|The Island of Dr. Brain]], Moore helped create the game's distinctive visual identity.[^ref-2] The game featured Dr. Thaddeus Egghead Brain's private island, "filled with visual puns and a gauntlet of peculiar puzzles."[^ref-6] The art team's work supported educational puzzles spanning math, language, chemistry, art history, physics, logic, mechanics, music, genetics, literature, and navigation.[^ref-7]

## Legacy

Jerry Moore's career at Sierra exemplifies the multidisciplinary nature of early game development, where individuals contributed programming, art, or both depending on project needs.[^ref-1] His transition from programmer to artist reflects the evolving demands of the industry as graphics became increasingly important during the VGA era.

His work on educational titles like Donald Duck's Playground and The Island of Dr. Brain helped establish Sierra's presence in the edutainment market, creating games that entertained children while teaching valuable skills.[^ref-6] These titles demonstrated that Sierra's creative capabilities extended beyond the adventure games that defined the company's reputation.
